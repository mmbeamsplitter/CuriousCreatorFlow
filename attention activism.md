As many is entangled with today’s notion and playing field of attention, I think you will find also many interesting perspectives in previously planted Seeds - from [Fast Language](https://forum.protein.xyz/t/fast-language-ai-slob-the-tinkerbell-effect/415/9) to the [Need of friction](https://forum.protein.xyz/t/cultural-singularity-the-need-for-friction/429/3) and everything around [sense/meaning-making](https://forum.protein.xyz/t/goosebumps-in-the-synthocene-era/432).

**My contribution (hopefully :)) to this conversation:**  
Although I think initiatives such as The School of Radical Attention are definitely interesting and raise awareness, to me it sort of reveals once again how we’re always trying to fight fire with fire, or in this case, attention with radical attention? Not really willing to address the painful and uncomfortable mess beneath it all: as in first putting out the fire that makes us feel warm and safe …

I think a big mistake we might be making is that the ‘Attention economy’ and today’s fight - by influencers, protest movements, click-baits, and populist agendas - for our limited mental bandwidth is something new. I even believe the *Intention Economy* is already lining up to take over if we don’t pay enough attention to it ;)… Which is kind of the attention economy on steroids, embedded in time (future).

**Attention Economy: as old as ancient times**  
For more than a decade, we’ve seen the silent rise, dedicated trend rapports, public embrace and now alarm bells regarding the Attention Economy — the race to capture, commodify, exploit, and controle our (self-indulgent) focus through engaging content and addictive platforms. Yet, if we look at media, politics, and culture through the lens of (h)istory, the attention economy is far older than the era of social media. It draws on dynamics that have shaped human gatherings, propaganda campaigns, and even the earliest forms of public spectacle. Well, in all honestly …human language; how we interact with the world, … The commodification of our attention, the interplay between who gets and who pays attention is a game as old as ancient times. The tactics we see today on social networks are - in my opinion - digital upgrades of techniques as old as oratory and spectacle itself.

**‘Lucky Strike’ Edward Bernays**, often called the ‘father of public relations’ demonstrated in the early 20th century that tapping into emotional triggers — rather than logical deliberation — could shape public opinion at mass scale. To give you an idea; he ended his book Propaganda (at that time a different meaning than now): *Propaganda will never die out. Intelligent men must realize that propaganda is the modern instrument by which they can fight for productive ends and help to bring order out of chaos…*

In dark/volatile times, fear, uncertainty AND emotions run high — and often distract and ‘trump’ reasoned argument. Anthropologist **Victor Turner’s** work on not only ‘structure and anti-structure/liminality’ but also ‘social dramas’ highlights how communities become captivated by drama, crisis — conflict-as-performance — suggesting that conflict and spectacle function as (cultural) magnets for attention. This phenomenon persists because attention is a finite resource, tightly bound to survival instincts (**Kahneman**, Attention and Effort, 1973), and as such, easily hijacked.

Whether it’s a ‘distracting and shine-bright-like-a-star light’ or a dark/black-out narrative, we’re now aware - backed-up by enough research…- how our attention can be directed, redacted and used to serve particular ends that might end up harming us. If the majority fixates its attention on a slogan, sound, hero, belief, or meme, coin,… it most likely becomes *real*, exerting force on elections, markets, or cultural norms. And historically, those best at harnessing the ‘spectacle’ have often shaped events and (h)istories in profound ways. Now let’s all clap for Tinkerbell?

**Friction: not attention’s best friend**  
Hence also today’s focus on interconnectedness, UX, infinite scrolling, unlimited access, integration etc &gt; to facilitate a frictionless sea for an endlessly flow of attention…However, friction — whether it comes from skeptical critique, dislike, contradictory sources, or slower, more deliberative forms of engagement — acts as a necessary filter, but of course a big hump on the ‘Attention road’. Previous Seed conversation regardin the Need for Friction suggests that without friction, messages move too swiftly… to unattended through society. A bit of slowness, or friction, fosters reflection, which can counterbalance the raw speed of today’s attention-grabing meme-like culture.

**Moreover, friction has cultural value**. As anthropologists like Mary Douglas have argued (Purity and Danger , 1966), societies negotiate order and disorder through human-made (artificial) boundary/meaning-making processes. Whether in the name of truth or purity… These boundaries — rules, norms, word views or beliefs — are forms of friction that keep certain harmful ‘means’ from gaining attention and traction too quickly.

**The question we might need to address is Why and for what are we (not) clapping?**

**Next stop: The Intention Economy**  
As mentioned in the beginning, I believe we’re already in an era where individual intent, not attention, is becoming the driving force in marketplaces. Aka: straight to the source, directly plugged-in… 100% frictionless. Although the intention economy has the promise to flip the script - as described by **Doc Searls** ( The Intention Economy: When Customers Take Charge, 2012 ) To me, it’s also the last ‘frixtion’ that guards choice and agency, knowing the rooted powers of big tech and attention-seeking, data-rich businesses.

**In many ways, we’re at an inflection point.** Will the attention economy, with its hyper-x thirst for clicks, outrage, and viral sensations, further weave itself into cultural discourse? Or will an intention ‘economy’ — rooted in autonomy, conscious choice, and mutual benefit — gain momentum?

**I always come back to two sentences when it comes to real change:**

- Where the mind goes, energy flows. Or as I also say (E=mc2) &gt; Power = Intention * Attention
- Those who have privilege are not going to give it up without a fight - Noam Chomsky

I can also recommend the Center for Humane Technology [Undivided Attention Podcast](https://www.humanetech.com/podcast)
