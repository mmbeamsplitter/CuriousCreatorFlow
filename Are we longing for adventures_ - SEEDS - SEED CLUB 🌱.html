I’ve been thinking about how much we optimize for Attention. Look at Sora and Likee. Attention is quickly becoming a race to the bottom.

I’m curious what the next sequence in the funnel needs to look like right now. Most of us don’t seem to be suffering from a lack of information. But I’m starting to notice a real hunger for different kinds of formation – signals that point us somewhere and give us a story to step into, not just capture us and telling uswhat to react to. To me this feels like something more akin to adventure. And I think that gap between capturing and inviting is where a lot of brands and leaders are losing people.

I wonder if the nostalgia wave we’re swimming in right now is actually a sign post. Maybe people aren’t just longing for the past, they’re longing for a time when things felt like there were these big, sweeping stories to be a part of.

At least here in the states, political movements (whether it’s Mamdani’s campaign on one side or Turning Point on the other) have tapped into the desire to socialize offline and create things together that feels more like meaningful artifacts than entertainment. What are the implications for how brands think about activation altogether? Is it crazy to think culture’s desire for uninterrupted content may soon be outmatched by its desire for something to actually do?

Even mild adventures, things that feel within reach, still carry meaning. And I wonder if the communicators who figure out how to send people somewhere, rather than just stop them in their tracks, are the ones who end up earning something more valuable, something closer to commitment.

Absolutely! Touched on related topics in Keeping Secrets: IYKYK and "Great things start in little rooms" . I think there’s no question that people whether they realise it or not are craving the uniqueness and unpredictability of experience that can only come from not being in a gated, wristbanded, signposted-fire-exits, pre-packaged, pre-planned “experience®”. In fact, the fact that “experience” has now come to mean exactly that - something you can log on and buy for your brother-in-law in a choice of pricing terriers - kind of tells us exactly where we’re at, right?

But I also keep coming back to the quote from Noz that first inspired Culture as Casino [SEED #8337]

“How can we lure men back to the arts when the financial and dopamine incentives for outrage and gambling are so much higher?”

And for “the arts” we could of course sub in any number of pro-social things - volunteering, family, egalitarian politics, community, participatory scenes etc. And reading your SEED this morning, I had a chilling realisation that the anti-social, individualist, Dark Mode, extractive, post-truth sides of society DO offer - and always have offered - adventure. Fraternities hazing and operating above the law. The Proud Boys. Mercenaries. Passport bros. Gang members. Crypto nomads. Oligarchs and their fucking spaceships. All of these are in some sense seeking the kind of adventure the “running away to sea” or “seeking your fortune” used to in times gone by. Hell, even the trad wife aesthetic is telling you that you can have a storybook life.

So yeah, 100%, how can the sense of adventure be attached tot things that don’t destroy us?

There’s something, too about how the fact the weather is never going to be the same again - that we’re in a time of floods and fires and hurricanes - that is going to bring back adventure whether we like it or not. Likewise the ever increasing background presence of mysterious AI and cults around it (which is of course one of the reasons We've Never Needed Sci-fi More [SEED #8312 + #8313] lol). It’s gonna be a wild ride, so we probably have to embrace that!
