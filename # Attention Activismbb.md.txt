### Attention Economy: As Old as Ancient Times

For more than a decade, we've seen the silent rise, dedicated trend reports, public embrace and now alarm bells regarding the Attention Economy—the race to capture, commodify, exploit, and control our (self-indulgent) focus through engaging content and addictive platforms.

Yet, if we look at media, politics, and culture through the lens of history, the attention economy is far older than the era of social media. It draws on dynamics that have shaped human gatherings, propaganda campaigns, and even the earliest forms of public spectacle. The commodification of our attention, the interplay between who gets and who pays attention is a game as old as ancient times. The tactics we see today on social networks are digital upgrades of techniques as old as oratory and spectacle itself.

**Edward Bernays**, often called the "father of public relations," demonstrated in the early 20th century that tapping into emotional triggers—rather than logical deliberation—could shape public opinion at mass scale. He ended his book *Propaganda*: "Propaganda will never die out. Intelligent men must realize that propaganda is the modern instrument by which they can fight for productive ends and help to bring order out of chaos."

In dark and volatile times, fear, uncertainty, and emotions run high—and often distract and "trump" reasoned argument. Anthropologist **Victor Turner's** work on "structure and anti-structure/liminality" and "social dramas" highlights how communities become captivated by drama and crisis—conflict-as-performance—suggesting that conflict and spectacle function as cultural magnets for attention. This phenomenon persists because attention is a finite resource, tightly bound to survival instincts (**Kahneman**, *Attention and Effort*, 1973), and as such, easily hijacked.

Whether it's a distracting bright light or a dark blackout narrative, we're now aware—backed up by enough research—how our attention can be directed, redacted, and used to serve particular ends that might end up harming us. If the majority fixates its attention on a slogan, sound, hero, belief, meme, or coin, it most likely becomes *real*, exerting force on elections, markets, or cultural norms. Historically, those best at harnessing the "spectacle" have often shaped events and histories in profound ways.

### Friction: Not Attention's Best Friend

Hence today's focus on interconnectedness, UX, infinite scrolling, unlimited access, integration—to facilitate a frictionless sea for an endless flow of attention. However, friction—whether it comes from skeptical critique, dislike, contradictory sources, or slower, more deliberative forms of engagement—acts as a necessary filter, but of course a big hurdle on the "Attention road."

Without friction, messages move too swiftly, too unattended through society. A bit of slowness, or friction, fosters reflection, which can counterbalance the raw speed of today's attention-grabbing meme-like culture.

**Moreover, friction has cultural value.** As anthropologists like **Mary Douglas** have argued (*Purity and Danger*, 1966), societies negotiate order and disorder through human-made boundary/meaning-making processes. Whether in the name of truth or purity, these boundaries—rules, norms, worldviews, or beliefs—are forms of friction that keep certain harmful "means" from gaining attention and traction too quickly.

**The question we might need to address is: Why and for what are we (not) clapping?**

### Next Stop: The Intention Economy

I believe we're already in an era where individual intent, not attention, is becoming the driving force in marketplaces. Straight to the source, directly plugged-in, 100% frictionless.

Although the intention economy has the promise to flip the script—as described by **Doc Searls** (*The Intention Economy: When Customers Take Charge*, 2012)—to me, it's also the last "friction" that guards choice and agency, knowing the rooted powers of big tech and attention-seeking, data-rich businesses.

**In many ways, we're at an inflection point.** Will the attention economy, with its hyper-thirst for clicks, outrage, and viral sensations, further weave itself into cultural discourse? Or will an intention economy—rooted in autonomy, conscious choice, and mutual benefit—gain momentum?
